Anger & Frustration

It feels as if your body has reacted to perceived unfairness, obstacles, or blocked goals. Your heart beats faster, your muscles tense, and your mind sharpens its focus on the source of irritation.

Anger

Reacting strongly to perceived threat, injustice, or violation.

Anger

Feelings

Frustration, irritation, hostility, tension, and intense focus on the source of anger. Your heart rate increases, muscles tighten, and the body prepares for confrontation.

Brain

The amygdala detects threat or unfairness and rapidly activates emotional responses. The hypothalamus initiates the fight-or-flight response, while the prefrontal cortex may struggle to inhibit impulsive reactions in the moment.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

Adrenaline and cortisol surge, increasing energy, alertness, and readiness to act. Serotonin may temporarily drop, reducing emotional regulation.

Real-life Scenarios

Being blamed unfairly, someone cutting in line, repeated disrespect, or a teammate taking credit for your work.

Pause and breathe slowly: inhale for 4, hold for 2, and exhale for 6. This lowers adrenaline and gives the prefrontal cortex time to regain control before reacting.

Frustration

Feeling blocked despite effort or progress.

Frustration

Feelings

Restlessness, impatience, tension, mental fatigue, and irritation when obstacles prevent forward movement.

Brain

The anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) detects conflict between expectations and reality. The amygdala heightens attention toward obstacles, increasing emotional pressure.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

Cortisol rises to maintain focus; adrenaline increases motivation to push through the challenge.

Real-life Scenarios

Slow internet during work, repeated interruptions, unclear instructions, or a project that won’t move forward.

Calming Point

Break the task into one tiny, achievable step. Completing it restores control and reduces ACC-detected conflict.

Resentment

Sustained anger held long after the triggering event.

Resentment

Feelings

Bitterness, grudges, emotional heaviness, and repeated mental replay of unfair situations.

Brain

The hippocampus stores emotional memories of injustice, while the amygdala reactivates anger whenever the memory is recalled.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

Chronically elevated cortisol maintains stress; reduced serotonin can worsen mood regulation and emotional flexibility.

Real-life Scenarios

A friend repeatedly crossing boundaries, unresolved conflicts, or ongoing unfair treatment.

Calming Point

Write down what happened and how it affected you. Externalizing the memory reduces emotional replay and stress activation.

Irritation

Brief anger triggered by minor annoyances.

Irritation

Feelings

Quick spikes of annoyance, impatience, and mild tension that fade relatively fast.

Brain

The amygdala reacts instantly, but the prefrontal cortex quickly regains control once the stimulus passes.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

A short-lived increase in adrenaline and noradrenaline.

Real-life Scenarios

Pen tapping, background noise, small interruptions, or minor inconveniences.

Calming Point

Label the feeling silently (“This is irritation”) and take one slow breath. Naming emotions reduces their intensity.

Contempt

Feeling disdain, superiority, or emotional distance toward others.

Contempt

Feelings

Dismissiveness, judgment, moral superiority, emotional coldness, and lack of empathy rather than explosive anger.

Brain

The amygdala evaluates social threat or violation, while the prefrontal cortex engages in judgment. Reduced activity in empathy-related regions (including parts of the cingulate gyrus) can make emotional distancing easier.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

Lower oxytocin (reduced bonding), altered serotonin, and mild cortisol elevation maintain emotional detachment.

Real-life Scenarios

Looking down on someone’s mistakes, dismissing others’ opinions, or silently judging people you disagree with.

Calming Point

Take one breath and ask: “What might be happening for them that I don’t see?” Perspective-taking reactivates empathy circuits.

Find the Calm in the Middle of Chaos

When anger or frustration rises, try these simple techniques to cool down, release tension, and regain focus.

Quick Calm Reset (Anger)

What to do: Pause immediately. Take 3 slow breaths: inhale for 4, hold for 2, exhale for 6. Relax your jaw and shoulders as you exhale.

Why it works: Slow exhalation activates the parasympathetic nervous system, lowering adrenaline and helping the prefrontal cortex regain control over emotional impulses.

Quick tip: Unclench your jaw and drop your shoulders — anger often hides there.

What to do: Press your feet firmly into the ground or grip a solid object for 10 to 15 seconds while breathing slowly.

Why it works: Physical grounding shifts attention away from emotional triggers and signals safety to the brain, reducing amygdala activation.

Quick tip: Name one thing you can feel strongly (floor, chair, table) to anchor your attention.

Pause & Reframe (Frustration)

What to do: Ask yourself: “What part of this can I control right now?” Then act on just that piece.

Why it works: Engages the prefrontal cortex, reduces conflict detected by the anterior cingulate cortex, and restores a sense of control.

Quick tip: If you feel stuck, shrink the task until it feels almost too easy.

What to do: Tense your fists, shoulders, or legs for 5 seconds and then release fully. Repeat 2 to 3 times.

Why it works: Releases stored muscle tension and burns off excess adrenaline generated during anger or frustration.

Quick tip: Exhale strongly as you release to deepen the calming effect.

Cognitive Cool-Down (Resentment)

What to do: Write one sentence finishing: “What upset me was…” Then one sentence: “What I need now is…”

Why it works: Externalizing thoughts reduces emotional replay by the hippocampus and calms amygdala reactivation.

Quick tip: You don’t need solutions — clarity alone reduces emotional intensity.

Let-It-Pass Reset (Irritation)

What to do: Silently label the feeling: “This is irritation.” Take one slow breath and redirect attention.

Why it works: Naming emotions activates regulatory brain regions and prevents minor anger from escalating.

Quick tip: Most irritation fades in under 90 seconds if not fueled by thought.
