Stress & Overwhelm

Feeling like the demands of life are too much to handle at once? Your mind races and your body reacts.

Stress

Feeling tense, pressured, and restless.

Stress

Feelings

Anxious, jittery, restless, mentally exhausted, emotionally drained, and physically tense. Your heart races and muscles tighten as you feel under constant pressure.

Brain

The amygdala heightens alertness and perceives threats, while the hypothalamus triggers the body’s stress response, preparing you to react to challenges.

Neurotransmitters

Cortisol surges, giving your body energy, and adrenaline sharpens your focus, making you hyper-aware of your surroundings.

Real-life Scenarios

Facing looming deadlines, juggling multiple responsibilities, dealing with unexpected obstacles, or preparing for exams that feel overwhelming.

Quick Tip

Take three slow, deep breaths. Inhale for 4, hold for 2, exhale for 6. Repeat 3 times to calm your nervous system.

Overwhelm

Feeling swamped, confused, and exhausted.

Overwhelm

Feelings

Mentally scattered, stressed, panicked, overburdened, exhausted, and unable to focus. You feel pulled in multiple directions at once.

Brain

The amygdala intensifies emotional responses while the prefrontal cortex struggles to organize and prioritize thoughts, making decision-making difficult.

Neurotransmitters

Cortisol and norepinephrine spike, flooding your body with stress signals and making concentration almost impossible.

Real-life Scenarios

Multiple homework assignments, back-to-back exams, social obligations, or managing conflicting responsibilities all at once.

Quick Tip

Break tasks into tiny, achievable steps. Focus on completing one small thing first to regain control and reduce mental clutter.

Worried

Feeling anxious, uneasy, and preoccupied.

Worried

Feelings

Uneasy, apprehensive, nervous, restless, overthinking, and mentally spinning. Repetitive thoughts keep replaying, creating a sense of impending doom.

Brain

The amygdala amplifies perceived threats, while the prefrontal cortex struggles to plan effectively, often creating repetitive thought loops.

Neurotransmitters

Cortisol rises, heightening alertness; serotonin and GABA try to stabilize mood and calm the nervous system.

Real-life Scenarios

Obsessing over past conversations, constantly checking messages, or anticipating every possible mistake or negative outcome.

Quick Tip

Write down worries and set them aside. Helps your mind reset and reduces mental looping.

Anxiety

Feeling nervous, tense, and restless.

Anxiety

Feelings

Restless, jittery, tense, uneasy, apprehensive, and on edge. Thoughts constantly jump to “what if” scenarios.

Brain

The amygdala detects potential threats, the hypothalamus triggers the fight-or-flight response, and the prefrontal cortex struggles to reason clearly under pressure.

Neurotransmitters

Cortisol and adrenaline rise, GABA decreases, and serotonin fluctuates, making you feel restless and apprehensive.

Real-life Scenarios

Feeling nervous before a test, big presentation, or an unfamiliar event. Worrying excessively about outcomes.

Quick Tip

Try the 5-4-3-2-1 grounding technique: notice 5 things you see, 4 things you touch, 3 things you hear, 2 things you smell, and 1 thing you taste.

Avoidance

Trying to escape tasks that make you anxious.

Avoidance

Feelings

Anxious, nervous, hesitant, conflicted, and temporarily relieved by avoidance. Stress remains beneath the surface.

Brain

The amygdala signals danger, while the prefrontal cortex may rationalize skipping tasks, reinforcing avoidance patterns.

Neurotransmitters

Dopamine provides temporary reward and relief, linking avoidance to short-term comfort.

Real-life Scenarios

Skipping assignments, delaying difficult conversations, avoiding social interactions, or procrastinating important tasks.

Quick Tip

Pick one small task today and face it. Small wins reduce avoidance habits and boost confidence.

Find the Calm in the Storm

Feeling overwhelmed or stressed? Here are playful, simple, and effective ways to reset your body and mind.

Quick Calm Reset

What to do: Take three slow, deep breaths. Feel your chest rise and fall. Exhale slowly, letting your shoulders drop.

Why it works: Deep breathing lowers heart rate, reduces cortisol, and helps your body enter a calmer state.

Quick tip: Count to 4 on inhale, hold 2, exhale 6. Repeat 3 times.

Grounding Exercise

What to do: Use the 5-4-3-2-1 exercise: notice 5 things you see, 4 things you feel, 3 things you hear, 2 things you smell, 1 thing you taste.

Why it works: Focusing on your senses pulls attention away from anxious thoughts, helping you feel present and safe.

Quick tip: Move slowly and notice small details you usually ignore.

Tiny Steps Technique

What to do: Break tasks into tiny steps: "Write one sentence," or "Start the timer for 5 minutes."

Why it works: Small steps make tasks feel manageable and prevent overwhelm. Completing tiny goals boosts confidence.

Quick tip: Celebrate each small win to reinforce progress.

Body Reset

What to do: Stretch your arms, shake your muscles, or take a short walk or gentle yoga session.

Why it works: Physical movement releases built-up tension, lowers adrenaline, and signals your brain to calm down.

Quick tip: Roll your shoulders and gently rotate your neck to release stiffness.

Mind Recharge

What to do: Drink water, rest for 10 minutes with eyes closed, or listen to calming music.

Why it works: Hydration and short breaks reset your nervous system, reduce stress hormones, and improve focus.

Quick tip: Try a mindful sip of water: notice its temperature and taste while breathing deeply.
