Guilt & Self-Consciousness

Feeling like your actions, appearance, or choices are being judged and that you have done something wrong or fallen short.

Embarrassment

Feels like: Your face feels hot, your stomach twists, and you wish you could disappear.

Embarrassment

Feelings

Awkward, shy, flustered, wanting to hide, fidgety.

Brain

The amygdala senses social threats, the anterior cingulate cortex notices self-evaluation, and the prefrontal cortex tries to control your reaction.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

Adrenaline causes blushing and fast heartbeat. Cortisol rises if the situation lasts.

Real-life Examples

Tripping in class, saying something silly in a presentation, spilling food in front of friends.

Quick Tip

Take a deep breath, remind yourself everyone makes mistakes, and focus on moving forward.

Shame

Feels like: Feeling like you are “not good enough” or “a failure.”

Shame

Feelings

Small, exposed, guilty, self-critical, wanting to disappear.

Brain

The amygdala amplifies feeling judged, the prefrontal cortex struggles to regulate self-critical thoughts, and the cingulate cortex processes social pain.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

Low serotonin reduces mood stability. Cortisol rises when feeling judged or watched.

Real-life Examples

Failing an important test, getting teased, forgetting something important in public.

Quick Tip

Write down one good thing about yourself. Focus on your strengths, not mistakes.

Guilt

Feels like: “I shouldn’t have done that” or “I should have done better.”

Guilt

Feelings

Regret, responsibility, tension, motivation to fix things.

Brain

The amygdala signals social threat, the prefrontal cortex helps think about right and wrong, and the hippocampus remembers past actions.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

Cortisol rises under stress; dopamine drops until you take action to make things right.

Real-life Examples

Forgetting a friend’s birthday, breaking a promise, hurting someone by accident.

Quick Tip

Apologize or do a small action to fix the situation. It helps reduce guilt and makes you feel better.

Humiliation

Feels like: Feeling completely exposed or ridiculed in front of others.

Humiliation

Feelings

Shame, embarrassment, anger, wanting to hide or escape.

Brain

The amygdala reacts strongly to social threats, the prefrontal cortex struggles to regulate shame, and the hypothalamus triggers stress responses.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

Cortisol and adrenaline spike. Your heart races, and your face might flush.

Real-life Examples

Being laughed at, harsh criticism in public, or being embarrassed online.

Quick Tip

Step away, take slow breaths, and remind yourself this moment does not define you.

Comparison-Anxiety

Feels like: Constantly thinking “I’m not as good as them” or “I’ll never measure up.”

Comparison-Anxiety

Feelings

Insecure, nervous, self-conscious, stressed, and jealous.

Brain

The amygdala detects social threats, the prefrontal cortex ruminates on comparisons, and the ventral striatum responds to social rewards.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

Low serotonin affects mood, cortisol rises, dopamine fluctuates depending on how you see others’ success.

Real-life Examples

Scrolling social media, comparing grades, looks, or popularity.

Quick Tip

Limit time on social media and write down your own achievements. Focus on yourself.

Find the Calm in the Middle of Guilt & Self-Consciousness

When feelings of embarrassment, shame, or guilt feel strong, try these techniques to calm your mind, regain confidence, and feel more in control.

Practice Self-Compassion

What to do: Say kind words to yourself: “It’s okay. Everyone makes mistakes.”

Why it works: Calms the amygdala and reduces self-criticism.

Quick Tip: Place a hand over your heart while saying one kind phrase.

Ground Yourself in the Present

What to do: Use your senses: notice 5 things you see, 4 you feel, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste.

Why it works: Stops your mind from spinning on embarrassment, shame, or guilt.

Quick Tip: Move slowly, notice details, and focus on now.

Reframe the Situation

What to do: Instead of “I messed up,” try “I can learn and do better next time.”

Why it works: Engages the prefrontal cortex to reduce stress and negative thinking.

Quick Tip: Write one positive lesson from the situation.

Connect with Trusted People

What to do: Talk or spend quiet time with someone you trust.

Why it works: Boosts oxytocin, lowers loneliness, and calms fear of judgment.

Quick Tip: Send a message or sit with a friend: small moments help.

Physical Body Reset

What to do: Stretch, shake arms, roll shoulders, or walk.

Why it works: Releases adrenaline, reduces tension, and improves mood.

Quick Tip: Notice the breeze outside or sunlight while moving.

Journaling & Reflection

What to do: Write down your feelings, what caused them, and one small action to feel better.

Why it works: Organizes thoughts and reduces emotional intensity.

Quick Tip: Even a few lines help your mind feel lighter.
