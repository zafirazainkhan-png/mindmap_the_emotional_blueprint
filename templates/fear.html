Fear & Vulnerability

Your body and brain reacting to possible danger, uncertainty, or feeling exposed. Heart races, muscles tense, and your mind focuses on staying safe.

Fear

Strong reaction to a real or imagined threat.

Fear

Feelings

Heart racing, sweating, tense muscles, urge to escape or freeze.

Brain

The amygdala quickly detects danger and triggers the fight-or-flight response. The hypothalamus activates stress hormones, while the prefrontal cortex tries to assess if the threat is real.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

Adrenaline and cortisol surge for instant energy and alertness.

Real-life Scenarios

Sitting in the dentist’s chair before a procedure, hearing a strange noise at night, or speaking in front of the class.

Slow deep breaths: inhale for 4, hold for 2, and exhale for 6. This tells your amygdala the danger is passing.

Insecurity

Feeling unsure about yourself or your worth.

Insecurity

Feelings

Self-doubt, worry about being judged, feeling “not good enough.”

Brain

The amygdala senses social threat, the prefrontal cortex overthinks comparisons, and the cingulate gyrus processes feelings of exclusion.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

Lower serotonin can worsen self-doubt; cortisol rises with social worry.

Real-life Scenarios

Comparing yourself on social media, worrying about fitting in with a new group, or doubting your abilities before a test.

Calming Point

Remind yourself of one strength or past success. Focusing on what you do well calms the social-threat response.

Helplessness

Feeling unable to change or control a difficult situation.

Helplessness

Feelings

Heavy, stuck, low energy, believing nothing you do will help.

Brain

The amygdala amplifies stress signals, the prefrontal cortex struggles with problem-solving, and the ventral striatum reduces feelings of reward or control.

Neurotransmitters & Hormones

Low dopamine lowers motivation; prolonged cortisol keeps the body in stress mode.

Real-life Scenarios

Feeling overwhelmed by schoolwork that keeps piling up, being in a tough family situation, or dealing with something that feels out of your control.

Calming Point

Find one small thing you can control (tidy your desk, drink water, text a friend). Tiny actions rebuild a sense of power and dopamine.

Find the Calm in the Middle of Chaos

When fear, insecurity, or helplessness feel strong, try these simple techniques to calm your mind, regain confidence, and feel more in control.

Deep Breathing

What to do: Inhale slowly for 4, hold for 2, exhale for 6. Repeat 5 times.

Why it works: Slow breathing calms the amygdala and lowers adrenaline fast.

Quick tip: Place a hand on your belly to feel it rise and fall.

Anchor with Senses

What to do: Name 5 things you see, 4 you can touch, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste.

Why it works: Brings attention to the present, reducing fear of future threats.

Quick tip: Do it slowly: really notice each detail.

Challenge Fearful Thoughts

What to do: Ask: “Is this thought 100% true?” or “What would I tell a friend?”

Why it works: Activates the prefrontal cortex to question amygdala-driven fears.

Quick tip: Example: “Failing one quiz doesn’t mean I’ll fail the whole subject.”

Seek Connection & Comfort

What to do: Reach out to someone safe: talk, hug, or just sit together.

Why it works: Boosts oxytocin, signals safety, and reduces vulnerability feelings.

Quick tip: Even texting “I’m feeling off” can help.

Body Reset

What to do: Stretch, shake out arms/legs, or take a short walk.

Why it works: Releases built-up adrenaline and tension from fear response.

Quick tip: Shake gently like a dog shaking off water: it is surprisingly effective!

Self-Compassion Pause

What to do: Place hand on heart and say: “This is hard right now, and that’s okay.”

Why it works: Activates soothing system and reduces self-critical fear loops.

Quick tip: Speak to yourself like you would to a younger sibling or close friend.
